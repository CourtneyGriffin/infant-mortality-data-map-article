The Hidden Driver of Infant Mortality In New York City's Underserved Communities

Study Reveals Alarming Infant Mortality Disparities Across New York City's Community Districts, Driven by Discriminatory Funding

by Courtney Griffin
December 20, 2024

Infant mortality has been a longstanding crisis in New York City, disproportionately impacting low-income and minority communities.

A study by the Department of Health & Mental Hygiene, using data averaged between 2018 and 2020, revealed significant disparities in infant mortality rates across the city’s community districts, with the highest rates in underserved communities.



Michele Akosua Chin-On, co-founder of Our Bodies Our Births After Birth Initiative, links this critical issue to redlining, calling it a form of oppression and the “root” of infant mortality, with the latter serving as a “blossom” of this systemic problem.

“I'm not surprised the map shows that the families that live in many of these districts don't have access to more than adequate medical care,” Chin-On said. “If the city can address the lack of equal distributions of funding, the infant mortality rate would drastically decrease and mothers can be spared the trauma of losing a child.”
